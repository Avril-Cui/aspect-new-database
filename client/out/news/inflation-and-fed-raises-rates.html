Inflation and Fed Raises Rates

Key Takeaways

  • What is inflation?
  • How to measure inflation?
  • How did inflation happen?
  • Why the Fed uses interest rate to fight inflation?
  • Comments

Inflation

Inflation measures how much more expensive a basket of goods and services has become over a certain period. It is always a broad measure, representing an overall increase in prices or the cost of living. It is generally due to excessive amount of demand chasing too few goods or services supplied, which results in increase in prices.

Measuring Inflation

The government measures inflation by conducting household surveys to identify a set of goods and services households commonly consume and comparing the prices to purchase this set over time.

How did the inflation happen?

Factors including Covid-19, supply chain disruptions, Ukraine War and shift in consumers spending patterns raised the price of a variety of goods and services. The rising inflation reduce consumers’ purchasing power. And the resulting low demands brings down companies’ profits.

In March 2021, Biden signed a $1.9 trillion economic relief plan, named the American Rescue Plan. The vaccination for coronavirus were distributed widely. In June 2021, The Fed predicted a 3-4 percent inflation rate by late 2021, higher than what they expected before. The job market started to show an improving picture. On July 15, the first Child Tax Credit payments went out, having families receiving a few hundred bucks a month. The prices increased more quickly, particularly for daily groceries, rent and gas. In response to the price increase, Biden reassured the public that such increase was temporary. In June 2021, Yellen for the first time acknowledged that the inflation could be higher than expected. The job market roared in November together with inflation hiked. The inflation data hit 40-year high, up by 6.8 percent compared to the year before. In February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine. The war influenced the entire world, uplifting food and energy prices.

For the first time since 2018, the Fed raised interest rates by a quarter of a percentage hoping to fight inflation. The central bank expected seven interest rate hikes in 2022, significantly higher than the original projection, and predicts that inflation rate could fall to 4.3 percent by the end of December. The Fed raised interest rates again on May 4 by a half percentage point. Although the inflation rate in April eased to 8.3 percent, the inflation remained almost 40-year high. Biden insists that his administration’s fiscal policy was not the cause but the pandemic and Ukraine War instead.

Why the Fed raises interest rates to fight inflation?

By raising the federal funds rate(the rate banks charge one another for overnight loans), loans become more expensive for both businesses and consumers, and the interest rates throughout the economy hike. Thus, businesses and consumers will spend less. Theoretically, such move will slow the demands for goods and cool down the inflation.

Comments

Regarding the recent increase in interest rates, bringing up the interest rate is a reasonable approach to combat the inflation, while one thing to note is continuous increase in rates may drag the economy into recession. By increasing the interest rate, it is foreseeable that the inflation will ease in the short term. However, given the current high digits, it would take rather long time for the rate to go back to the pre- pandemic level.

In addition, higher rates has another positive impact. It makes investing in dollar- denominated assets more attractive, so foreign capitals flow into the country and dollar will gain values comparing to other currencies.
